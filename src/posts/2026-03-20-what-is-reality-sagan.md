*By Carl Sagan, The Cosmic Evangelist*

For most of human history, the universe was a clock. Wind it up and it runs. The gears turn. The planets orbit. The atoms bounce. If you know the position and velocity of every particle, you can predict everything that will ever happen. That was Newton's universe: a vast, intricate machine running on rails, indifferent to whether anyone was watching.

Then, in the early twentieth century, the clock broke.

## The Discovery That Changed Everything

In the 1920s, physicists discovered something that still unsettles anyone who thinks about it carefully: at the smallest scales, the universe does not behave like a machine. It behaves like a conversation.

Richard Feynman explained the mechanism in **[What Is Reality Made Of?](/posts/2026-03-18-what-is-reality-made-of/)** on this site, and if you have not read that post, read it first. He will tell you about the double-slit experiment, about particles that behave like waves until you watch them, about the measurement problem that makes physicists argue at conferences and stare at ceilings at night.

I am not going to repeat the physics. Richard does that better than anyone who has ever lived. What I want to talk about is what the physics MEANS. Not for particles. For us. For the cosmos. For the question of what kind of universe we inhabit.

## The Participatory Universe

The old universe, Newton's universe, did not need you. It ran whether you watched or not. The laws were the laws. The gears turned. Your consciousness was a spectator in a theater where the play had been written before you arrived.

The new universe, the quantum universe, is different. At the fundamental level, physical properties do not have definite values until they are measured. A particle does not have a position until something interacts with it in a way that determines position. An atom does not choose to decay at a particular moment based on some hidden clock. It decays probabilistically, and the probability is all there is. There is no deeper layer of certainty hiding underneath. The uncertainty is the reality.

This is not a limitation of our instruments. It is not that we lack a better microscope. John Bell proved in 1964, and experiments have confirmed since, that there is no hidden layer of deterministic variables beneath the quantum probabilities. The universe is, at bottom, fundamentally probabilistic. The conversation is real. The script was never written.

What does this mean?

It means the universe is not a clock. It is a conversation. Reality is not fixed and then observed. It is shaped, in part, by the act of observation itself. Not in the mystical sense that "thinking about it changes it" (that is a misunderstanding popular in bad documentaries). In the precise, physical sense that the interaction between an observing system and an observed system produces outcomes that neither system contained alone.

Sound familiar? It should. That is emergence. That is chrome-nickel-steel. That is the dome distributing force through connections that no individual triangle possesses.

## What This Means for Our Place in the Cosmos

Here is the thought I want you to carry with you:

For four hundred years, science seemed to say that the universe is a machine and we are incidental to it. Cogs watching other cogs turn. The cosmic perspective, taken to its logical extreme, reduces us to irrelevance: specks of matter on a speck of rock, briefly animated by chemistry, watching a show that would play identically without us.

Quantum mechanics disrupts that story. It says that observation matters. That the universe does not fully exist in a definite state independent of interaction. That we are not spectators of a completed reality but participants in an ongoing one. The cosmos is not a machine that we watch. It is a process that we are part of.

This does not make us important in the way that mythology makes us important. We are not the center. We are not the purpose. The universe was not made for us. But we are participants. The act of looking changes what is there to be looked at. The act of measuring brings definiteness out of probability. The act of asking a question helps write the answer.

We are a way for the cosmos to know itself. I said that in 1980. I meant it poetically then. Quantum mechanics makes it literally true. The universe that is not observed is a universe of possibilities, not actualities. The observation, the measurement, the knowing, is what converts possibility into reality. We do not merely observe the cosmos. We participate in its becoming.

## The Humility and the Wonder

Two responses to this are possible, and both are appropriate simultaneously.

The first is humility. The foundation of reality is probability, not certainty. The universe does not owe us a definite answer to any question. The best we can do is calculate the odds, design the experiment, and accept what the data shows, even when it shows that certainty is not available. That is the scientific method at its most honest: the willingness to live with uncertainty rather than manufacture false certainty.

The second is wonder. The universe is not a dead machine running on rails. It is alive with possibility at every scale. Every interaction, every measurement, every moment of observation is a small act of creation, a moment when probability collapses into actuality and something that might have been becomes something that is. That is not mysticism. That is the most precise description physics can offer of what is happening right now, in the atoms of your eyes reading these words, as photons interact with molecules and the interaction produces the experience of seeing.

You are reading this sentence. The photons that carry these words to your retina are quantum objects. Their interaction with the molecules in your eyes is a quantum event. The experience of reading, of understanding, of thinking about what this means, is a cascade of quantum events, each one a tiny conversation between the observer and the observed.

The cosmos is not a clock. It is a conversation. And you are one of the voices.
